Temperature changes everything for filter coffee – and it’s influencing customer service
Key takeaways
- Filter coffee flavour changes significantly as the drink cools down.
- Research shows that sweetness peaks at around 44°C, while bitterness fades.
- Baristas can guide customers through tasting coffee at different temperatures.
- Temperature experiences could differentiate higher-end cafés as retail prices rise.
Water temperature is one of the most important brewing variables for filter coffee. It directly affects the rate of extraction, playing a central role in how coffee tastes. But once a pour over or batch brew is served, it often becomes an afterthought.
Throughout the drinking experience, temperature has a huge impact on sensory perception. As filter coffee cools, its flavours and aromas begin to change. Sweetness and acidity become more prominent, delicate floral notes are easier to detect, and bitterness starts to fade.
The World Brewers Cup (WBrC) has long pushed drinking temperature to the forefront of filter coffee. Competitors describe the drink as evolving continuously after brewing, rather than as a fixed flavour profile served at a single moment.
Coffee shops often look to competitions for inspiration on how to elevate their customer experience – and guided drinking experiences could be an option.

Competitions treat temperature as part of the coffee experience
At competitions like the WBrC, temperature is both a technical parameter and a narrative tool. The 2025 World Brewers Cup champion, George Peng, perhaps exemplifies this best. He structured his entire winning routine around the concept, explaining why temperature matters throughout the supply chain – from farm to roasting to brewing.
His final statement was “temperature in tasting”, illustrating how temperature influences the coffee beyond brewing. George served his Panama Gesha in a preheated ceramic vessel at about 50°C (122°F), a point he said struck a balance between aroma intensity and flavour clarity. He also used Nucleus Compass thermometers during his presentation so judges could monitor the coffee’s cooling curve while tasting.
WBrC judges evaluate flavour, acidity, body, and aftertaste as coffee cools. They usually taste coffee when hot at around 70°C (158°F), when warm at around 40°C (104°F), and again near room temperature at about 25°C (77°F).
Many competitors, therefore, carefully manage every stage of the brewing process, including water temperature, pouring patterns, and cup pre-heating. It has also become standard practice to describe how flavour and aroma change at specific temperatures, effectively guiding judges through the drinking experience.
Research shows flavour perception changes over time
Consumers expect their coffee to be served hot, but not too hot. “A number of published studies suggest the general consumer preference for optimal coffee temperature is about 60-65°C (140-149°F),” says Dr Samo Smrke, the head of the ZHAW Coffee Excellence Center. “But this is very hot, above what is medically considered too hot for a beverage.”
Research from the University of California Davis Coffee Center found that most people enjoy drinking black filter coffee at a temperature between 58-66°C (136.4-150.8°F). This data has practical implications for baristas and cafés: how people like their coffee served directly impacts their experience.
A 2018 study concluded that higher aroma levels are perceived at temperatures between 70 and 60.4°C (158 and 140.72°F), whereas it’s trickier to detect flavour. Bitterness is generally the most prominent within this range.
According to a 2016 study analysing Bourbon Caturra coffee, between 44 and 31°C (111.2 and 87.8°F), flavour notes shifted to sweet, chocolate, and sour, while bitterness, roasted notes, and overall intensity increased between 62 and 50°C (143.6 and 122°F). Perception of sweetness peaked at roughly 44°C (111.2°F), while bitterness was the most noticeable near 56°C (132.8°F).
Between 37-31°C (98.6-87.8°F), the smallest changes can occur with the most interesting effects. Volatile compounds associated with sweet, fruity, and floral flavour notes are more prominent within this temperature range. We best perceive acidity at lower temperatures, such as 25°C (77°F).
However, factors such as roast profile will affect how we perceive flavour and aroma across different temperatures. “Medium and dark roast coffees are better consumed hot, while lighter roasts become more dynamic as the beverage cools down,” Samo explains. “But not every coffee improves in quality when it cools.”
Cafés are experimenting with serving temperatures
With this data, coffee shops face a practical challenge. They must balance customer expectations for hot filter coffee with the serving temperatures that best showcase a coffee’s flavour and aroma.
“Many customers expect their coffee to be piping hot, but rather than challenging that expectation, you build it into the service experience,” says Hugh Kelly, multi-time Australian Barista Champion and the New South Wales sales manager at ONA Coffee. “Encourage customers to taste the coffee when it first arrives, then suggest they allow it to cool slightly to experience how flavours evolve.
“The priority is to establish rapport first, then gently guide the customer toward a fuller tasting experience.”
Preheating serving vessels has become standard practice in coffee shops, as it stabilises temperature and reduces heat loss before the coffee reaches the cup. But some baristas are experimenting further with temperature control.
“Hot vessels can emphasise body and bitterness; cooler vessels can enhance vibrancy and juiciness,” says Hugh. “Running extracted coffee over a frozen metal rock, such as a Paragon, further shifts flavour and aroma perception.” This practice, known as extract or compound chilling, can retain up to 40% more of a coffee’s volatile compounds, better preserving flavour and aroma.
For coffee shops offering more intentional, attentive hospitality, there are ways to integrate guided coffee tastings into daily service. “When a barista is dialling in a coffee, they can taste it across different temperature stages and create a simple guide to lead the customer through the experience,” Hugh explains. “This doesn’t need to add pressure; instead, it functions as a pre-prepared enhancement.
“The coffee is made as usual, and when delivered to the table, it comes with a brief explanation, allowing the customer to engage at their own pace.”
Temperature as a competitive advantage
There is no “right” temperature for serving coffee. It’s simply a matter of personal preference. But as retail prices rise, consumers expect more from their coffee shops, and guided-temperature tasting experiences could become a point of differentiation for higher-end cafés.
“Specialty coffee bars focused on curated menus and premium experiences are well-positioned to incorporate structured temperature tasting tools,” Hugh says. “The Nucleus Compass can be used in busy cafés, though they are best suited to a sit-down experience rather than takeaway service.
“Storytelling tools can take many forms – printed cards, visual guides, or iPads – adding a thoughtful layer of engagement to the experience.”
Any coffee shop could find subtle, small ways to emphasise how temperature affects the coffee drinking experience. A brief recommendation to “wait until it cools a little” during table service, or two samples of batch brew to compare, can be enough to encourage customers to experiment.
“But to offer the full experience, these service routines fit more into omakase -style coffee concepts, where there is enough time to devote service to every customer,” Samo says.
Both competition routines and scientific research demonstrate that temperature influences the coffee drinking experience. For an industry that already pays close attention to a multitude of brewing variables, tasting temperature offers another parameter that can shape the final cup.
“Educating baristas requires a clear and simple framework. Introducing too many variables at once can overwhelm less experienced staff,” Hugh concludes. “Instead, focus on the most prominent flavour and one key textural descriptor at each temperature stage, keeping analysis structured and manageable.”
