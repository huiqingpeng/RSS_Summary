Specialty coffee is taking over in Turkey
Key takeaways
- Turkey’s coffee culture is shifting beyond its 500-year-old cezve ritual.
- Turkish coffee remains a staple, but younger consumers are driving demand for espresso, filter coffee, and milk-based drinks.
- Specialty coffee culture is growing outside of Istanbul in cities like Ankara, Izmir, and Eskişehir.
- Turkish specialty brands like Espressolab are expanding aggressively into global markets.
Turkish culture has always been associated with the small, strong cup of coffee that bears its name. The 500-year-old tradition, brewed in a cezve or ibrik and served thick with sediment, is more than a beverage; it has earned UNESCO recognition as an Intangible Cultural Heritage.
“It’s a symbol of Turkish hospitality, accompanies conversation, marks the end of a meal, and initiates rest and repose,” says Tuncer Akgün, the CEO of Kurukahveci Mehmet Efendi, one of the world’s oldest roasters, founded in 1871.
However, its coffee culture is evolving. Across cities like Istanbul, Ankara, and Izmir, specialty coffee shops and roasters are opening alongside centuries-old coffeehouses. Turkish barista champions are competing on the international stage, and a new generation of young consumers is eager to explore new brewing methods.

Turkish and specialty coffee culture are co-existing
Although tea is the second-most-consumed beverage in Turkey, it has a long history of coffee consumption. The first coffeehouses opened in the mid-1500s in Istanbul, serving as key social spaces and quickly reshaping cultural life.
Today, Turkish coffee remains an important part of everyday life in the country, accounting for up to 70% of consumption. “It’s still the most popular form of coffee, although consumption of filter and espresso has grown rapidly in the past ten years,” Tuncer explains.
According to the International Coffee Organisation, coffee consumption in Turkey has increased by an average of 15.6% annually. Instant coffee accounts for much of this growth, but Turkish people are increasingly interested in a wider range of brewing methods.
“Turkish coffee isn’t meant to be consumed in a rush or on the go, so espresso and filter are welcome additions,” Tuncer explains. “An increasing number of people prefer different types of brews for different occasions throughout the day.”
Younger generations are driving the trend toward espresso, milk-based drinks, and filter coffee. This shift is most visible in major cities, where international coffee chains have accelerated consumer awareness and made Turkey one of the fastest-growing branded coffee markets in Europe.
“Although per capita coffee consumption in Turkey lags behind Europe, interest in specialty coffee is rapidly increasing,” says Taylan Kurt, the marketing manager at Spada Roastery in Istanbul, which opened a 5,300 sq ft facility in 2024 to expand roasting capacity. “For young consumers in particular, coffee is becoming a social space, a shared moment, and a form of personal expression. Factors like freshness, roast profile, sourcing, and brewing are also gaining more importance.”
The growth of specialty coffee beyond Istanbul
For years, Istanbul – the country’s largest city – was the centre of Turkey’s specialty coffee movement. “While it remains the largest and most developed coffee centre in Turkey, Ankara, as well as other cities such as Izmir and Eskişehir, are also developing into notable coffee hubs,” says Tuncer.
“We’re seeing a much stronger turnout at coffee festivals in Ankara, Izmir, Eskişehir, and Antalya in recent years. In all those places, enthusiastic young people are the main drivers.”
Ankara, the capital, has a growing network of roasters, shops, and barista competitors that mirrors Istanbul’s market. “Due to the city’s structure, character, and population, there are many boutique coffee shops, and they support each other through events and local competitions,” says Taylan.
Paper Roasting operates multiple cafés in Ankara, including a flagship roastery with a training lab. Roasters and coffee shops like Tetra N Roastery, PROD, ROR, and Rispetto are also introducing people to lighter roast profiles and new brewing methods.
Still, there are challenges to bridging the old and the new. “The concept of quality coffee isn’t yet widely accepted,” Taylan says. “There are still many customers who drink coffee as part of their daily routines without understanding or appreciating its quality. Some go to a café based on its decor, without understanding the coffee experience it provides.
“Improving this involves offering and explaining good coffee, moving beyond simply drinking it as a daily routine, focusing on cup quality, and brewing and serving coffee individually.”
High-quality Turkish coffee could be the answer, although more specialty coffee shops would need to invest in brewing equiptment. “The cezve/ibrik method isn’t usually available in many specialty coffee shops, so it’s rarely used as a stepping stone,” Taylan explains.
Turkish coffee brands expand internationally
As Turkey’s domestic specialty coffee market grows, brands are looking outwards. Istanbul’s Espressolab is the most obvious example. The specialty coffee roaster reached 400 stores globally by the end of 2025, after launching in countries such as Germany, Portugal, Morocco, the UAE, and Kazakhstan.
Other Istanbul brand 1401 Coffee also plans to expand internationally, aiming for 200 stores globally by 2030, with Germany, the UK, and the UAE targeted as key markets.
Turkish Barista and Coffee Roasting Champion Serkan Sagsoz opened Julith Coffee in Dubai. The omakase-style concept made headlines last year, when it purchased the 2025 Best of Panama-winning coffee for a record US$30,204/kg.
Kurukahveci Mehmet Efendi, meanwhile, exports to 60 countries, keeping Turkish coffee heritage alive. “We’re honoured to help share Turkish coffee in both existing and new markets,” Tuncer says.
Turkey’s domestic market for specialty coffee continues to expand, and several brands are now turning their focus to international growth. One of the most prominent examples is Espressolab, based in Istanbul. The coffee roaster reported operating 400 stores worldwide by the end of 2025, following launches in markets including Germany, Portugal, Morocco, the United Arab Emirates, and Kazakhstan.
Another Istanbul-based brand, 1401 Coffee, has also outlined international ambitions. The company says it plans to reach 200 stores globally by 2030, with Germany, the UK, and the UAE identified as key strategic markets.
Turkish barista champion Serkan Sagsoz has taken a different approach to international expansion. He opened Julith Coffee in Dubai, introducing an omakase-style coffee experience. The concept attracted attention in 2025 after purchasing the winning lot at the 2025 Best of Panama auction for a record price of US$30,204/kg.
What’s the future for Turkey’s specialty coffee market?
Accessibility is likely to remain a barrier in Turkey’s specialty coffee market for the foreseeable future. Between 2021 and 2025, the price of a cup of filter coffee increased by 780%, while a cup of Turkish coffee nearly doubled.
But as Turkey’s economy continues to grow, there’s plenty of potential for its specialty coffee market to continue thriving. Data from the World Coffee Portal show that the Turkish-branded coffee shop market grew 12.6% over the last five years, surpassing 3,800 outlets, despite a challenging economic climate.
Infrastructure is keeping pace with ambition. In 2022, Espressolab opened its Istanbul roastery – a 64,500 sq ft facility recognised as Europe’s largest coffee experience centre, welcoming over 10,000 visitors daily. It’s a statement of intent: Turkey wants to help define global specialty coffee culture.
Turkish coffee production
The country also has a unique advantage: it can grow coffee, albeit on a very small scale. For over 40 years, the Western Mediterranean Agricultural Research Center (BATEM) has attempted to cultivate coffee in Antalya as climate change heats up the region.
“We’ve been supporting an experimental coffee farm near Antalya for the past few years,” Tuncer says. “The farmer uses fully organic growing methods and cross plants with papaya and other tropical trees, which creates interesting flavour notes.
“Although most of the present yields are more suitable for filter coffees, we may be able to create a unique Turkish-grown Turkish coffee in the future,” Tuncer adds – a fittingly bold vision for a country that is simultaneously honouring its past and shaping the future of its coffee industry.
Turkey’s coffee market stands at a rare crossroads. Its deep-rooted cultural tradition gives it an authentic foundation, while a young, curious consumer base provides it with the energy to grow sustainably.
The cohort of specialty coffee shops and roasters, changing perceptions about quality and new ways to experience coffee, is culminating in an exciting market that will continue to thrive.
