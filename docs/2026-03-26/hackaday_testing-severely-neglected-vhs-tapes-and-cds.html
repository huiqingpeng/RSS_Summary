This article documents an experiment testing the playability of VHS tapes and CDs that had been abandoned on a derelict house porch for at least a decade, exposed to weather and animals. The experimenter cleaned these severely neglected physical media and attempted playback on older equipment to assess their actual durability.

This experiment reveals the real durability differences between storage media: CDs rely on a fragile reflective data layer that cannot recover from scratches, while VHS tapes—seemingly primitive—demonstrated remarkable environmental resilience. This offers insights for long-term data preservation strategies in the digital age and cautions against intuitive dismissal of "obsolete" technologies.
