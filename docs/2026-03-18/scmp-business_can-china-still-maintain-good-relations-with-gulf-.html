This article examines how Beijing's long-standing diplomatic strategy of maintaining friendly relations simultaneously with all six Gulf Cooperation Council (GCC) states and Iran is being severely tested by the recent escalation, where Iran struck targets in all GCC members in retaliation for US and Israeli attacks on Iran. This matters because: first, as the world's largest energy importer, China's energy security and economic interests are directly tied to Gulf stability; second, Beijing has sought to enhance its Middle East influence through a "non-aligned" posture, and losing this balance would damage its image as a "credible mediator"; third, prolonged conflict may force China to adjust its Belt and Road Initiative infrastructure investments in the region.
